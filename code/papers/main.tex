\begin{abstract}
Predicting human scanpaths in 360° omnidirectional images is a challenging task due to the large field of view, spherical geometry, and complex free-viewing behavior. Existing methods either rely on recurrent architectures that struggle with long-range dependencies, or fail to model the hierarchical nature of human visual exploration. In this paper, we propose \textbf{MambaGaze}, a novel scanpath prediction framework that integrates Mamba state-space models with a hierarchical saliency-guided sampling strategy for 360° panoramic images. Our model employs a two-phase exploration mechanism: an early global exploration phase that covers salient regions broadly, followed by a late refinement phase that focuses on locally prominent areas. Coordinate attention is incorporated into both the global and local visual encoders to enhance spatial awareness along horizontal and vertical directions. Extensive experiments on four benchmark datasets (Salient360, AOI, JUFE, and Sitzmann) demonstrate that MambaGaze consistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods in DTW and REC metrics, while achieving competitive Levenshtein distance scores. Ablation studies validate the contribution of each proposed component.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\IEEEPARstart{T}{he} rapid proliferation of 360° omnidirectional cameras and virtual reality (VR) head-mounted displays has created an urgent need to understand how humans visually explore panoramic scenes. Such understanding underpins a wide range of applications, including viewport-adaptive streaming, saliency-driven compression, immersive content creation, and VR quality assessment~\cite{sui2023scandmm,sitzmann2018saliency}. Unlike conventional 2D images, 360° panoramic images present unique perceptual challenges: the equirectangular field of view spans the full $360° \times 180°$ sphere, viewers actively rotate their heads to explore the scene, and the resulting gaze trajectories—known as scanpaths—exhibit complex, non-stationary spatial patterns that vary substantially across observers and scenes. Accurately modeling these patterns is therefore both scientifically important and practically valuable.

Scanpath prediction aims to generate a plausible sequence of fixation points that approximates the gaze trajectory of a human observer viewing a given image. Early approaches treated this as a spatial sampling problem, drawing fixations from a predicted saliency map~\cite{assens2017saltinet,monroy2018salnet360}. While saliency-based methods are intuitive, they fundamentally cannot capture the temporal dynamics of visual exploration: the order in which regions are visited, the inhibition of return to previously fixated locations, and the momentum of ongoing eye movements. These temporal dependencies are critical for generating realistic scanpaths that match human behavior.

To address the temporal dimension, more recent methods model scanpaths as sequential processes. Recurrent neural network (RNN) based approaches~\cite{xu2018predicting} maintain a hidden state that evolves with each fixation, but suffer from vanishing gradients over long sequences and tend to produce overly smooth, center-biased trajectories. Generative adversarial approaches such as PathGAN~\cite{assens2018pathgan} and ScanGAN~\cite{martin2022scangan} produce diverse scanpaths but lack explicit temporal structure and are difficult to train stably. The current state of the art, ScanDMM~\cite{sui2023scandmm}, proposes a deep Markov model that maintains a latent visual state $\mathbf{z}_t$ encoding a dynamic attention landscape, updated via a semantic-guided transition function and trained with a variational evidence lower bound (ELBO) objective. ScanDMM achieves strong performance across multiple benchmarks, but its first-order Markov structure constrains each state to depend only on the immediately preceding state, limiting the model's ability to capture long-range fixation dependencies. Furthermore, the variational training objective introduces considerable complexity, requiring careful tuning of KL annealing schedules and posterior approximations.

State space models (SSMs), and in particular the recently proposed Mamba architecture~\cite{gu2023mamba}, offer a compelling alternative for sequential modeling. Mamba employs an input-dependent selective scan mechanism that dynamically gates which information to retain or discard at each step, enabling efficient modeling of long-range dependencies with linear computational complexity in sequence length. Unlike Transformers, Mamba does not require quadratic attention computation, making it practical for real-time inference. Unlike RNNs, its parallel training formulation avoids vanishing gradients. These properties make Mamba well-suited for scanpath prediction, where fixation sequences can span 15–30 steps and long-range context (e.g., remembering which regions have already been visited) is essential for realistic trajectory generation.

However, directly applying Mamba to scanpath prediction ignores a key characteristic of human visual exploration: its hierarchical, two-phase structure. Neuroscience and eye-tracking studies consistently show that observers first conduct a rapid global survey of the scene, directing fixations toward broadly salient regions, before transitioning to a more focused refinement phase in which attention is concentrated on locally interesting areas~\cite{sui2023scandmm}. A model that treats all fixation steps uniformly fails to capture this natural progression from global to local exploration.

In this paper, we propose \textbf{MambaGaze}, a scanpath prediction framework that integrates Mamba sequence modeling with a biologically-motivated hierarchical sampling strategy for 360° panoramic images. Our approach makes three key contributions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item A \textbf{hierarchical saliency-guided sampling strategy} that explicitly models two-phase visual exploration: a high-exploration phase (60\% random exploration rate) covering the first 40\% of fixations for broad scene coverage, followed by a low-exploration phase (30\% rate) for the remaining fixations to refine attention on salient regions. Momentum-based trajectory smoothing ensures physically plausible step sizes and smooth gaze transitions between consecutive fixations.

  \item \textbf{Coordinate attention}~\cite{hou2021coordinate} integrated into both the global Glance encoder and the local Focus encoder, decomposing spatial attention into horizontal and vertical 1D encodings. This enables the model to precisely localize salient regions along both the azimuth and elevation axes of the equirectangular projection, which carry distinct semantic meaning in 360° viewing.

  \item A \textbf{Mamba-based sequence model}~\cite{gu2023mamba} that replaces the first-order Markov assumption of ScanDMM with a selective state-space formulation, allowing the model to condition each fixation prediction on the full history of previous fixations. This is trained end-to-end with a differentiable Soft-DTW loss~\cite{cuturi2017soft}, avoiding the complexity of variational inference.
\end{itemize}

We evaluate MambaGaze on four publicly available 360° scanpath benchmarks: Salient360~\cite{david2018dataset}, AOI~\cite{xu2018predicting}, JUFE~\cite{fang2022jufe}, and Sitzmann~\cite{sitzmann2018saliency}. The model is trained exclusively on Salient360 and evaluated zero-shot on the remaining three datasets, demonstrating strong cross-dataset generalization. MambaGaze consistently outperforms ScanDMM~\cite{sui2023scandmm} in DTW and REC metrics across all four datasets. Comprehensive ablation studies validate the contribution of each proposed component, and an analysis of model efficiency confirms that Mamba achieves superior performance without increasing inference latency relative to an LSTM baseline.

\section{Related Work}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Saliency-Based Methods}
Visual saliency prediction has a long history in computational vision~\cite{borji2013state}. Early bottom-up models identified low-level features such as color, contrast, and orientation as drivers of visual attention. Deep learning approaches~\cite{cornia2018predicting} significantly improved saliency prediction accuracy, and several works extended these to 360° images by accounting for spherical distortion~\cite{monroy2018salnet360}. However, saliency maps provide only a spatial probability distribution over fixation locations and do not model the temporal ordering of fixations. SaltiNet~\cite{assens2017saltinet} attempted to bridge this gap by sampling scanpaths from a time-dependent saliency volume, but the resulting paths exhibit unstable behavior with large displacements and sparse fixation clusters.

\subsection{Scanpath Prediction}
Scanpath prediction models the sequential nature of eye movements. IOR-ROI~\cite{sun2019visual} introduced inhibition of return and region-of-interest mechanisms to suppress previously visited locations. PathGAN~\cite{assens2018pathgan} used generative adversarial networks to produce diverse scanpaths for 2D images, later adapted to 360° images. ScanGAN~\cite{martin2022scangan} addressed spherical geometry by incorporating spherical CNNs and CoordConv layers with a spherical distance loss.

The most closely related work is ScanDMM~\cite{sui2023scandmm}, which proposes a deep Markov model for 360° scanpath prediction. ScanDMM maintains a latent visual state $\mathbf{z}_t$ that encodes a dynamic attention landscape, updated via a semantic-guided transition function $p_{\theta_t}(\mathbf{z}_t|\mathbf{z}_{t-1})$ and trained with a variational ELBO objective. A state initialization strategy anchors the starting fixation to the most salient region. While ScanDMM achieves strong performance, its first-order Markov structure limits the model to conditioning only on the immediately preceding state, and the variational training objective introduces significant complexity. Our work replaces the Markov chain with a Mamba SSM that directly models the full fixation history through selective state updates, and replaces the ELBO with a simpler Soft-DTW objective.

\subsection{State Space Models}
Structured state space models (S4)~\cite{gu2021efficiently} demonstrated strong performance on long-range sequence tasks by parameterizing continuous-time dynamics. Mamba~\cite{gu2023mamba} extended SSMs with input-dependent selective state transitions, enabling the model to focus on relevant context while filtering out noise, achieving competitive performance with Transformers at linear computational cost. Mamba has been applied to image recognition~\cite{zhu2024vision}, video understanding, and medical image segmentation, but its application to scanpath prediction remains unexplored prior to this work.

\subsection{Attention Mechanisms for Spatial Features}
Coordinate Attention~\cite{hou2021coordinate} decomposes channel attention into two 1D spatial encodings along height and width, enabling precise localization of spatial features. Unlike squeeze-and-excitation~\cite{hu2018squeeze} which aggregates global spatial information into a single descriptor, CoordAttention preserves directional spatial context. This is particularly beneficial for 360° panoramic images where horizontal (azimuth) and vertical (elevation) directions carry distinct semantic meaning related to viewing behavior.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
Given a 360° panoramic image $\mathbf{I} \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times H \times W}$ and its corresponding saliency map $\mathbf{S} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times H \times W}$, we aim to predict a scanpath $\hat{\mathbf{P}} = \{(\hat{x}_t, \hat{y}_t)\}_{t=1}^{T}$, where each $(\hat{x}_t, \hat{y}_t) \in [0,1]^2$ represents a normalized fixation coordinate in the equirectangular projection.

\subsection{Glance Network}
The Glance network $\mathcal{G}$ extracts a global scene representation from the full panoramic image. A global context vector is essential because human observers form an initial holistic impression of a scene before directing attention to specific regions—a phenomenon known as the ``gist'' of a scene. The Glance network consists of a convolutional encoder with two CoordAttention modules inserted after the second and third convolutional blocks:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{g} = \mathcal{G}(\mathbf{I}) \in \mathbb{R}^{d}
\end{equation}
where $d=256$ is the feature dimension. CoordAttention~\cite{hou2021coordinate} decomposes the attention into horizontal and vertical pooling branches, producing spatially-aware channel weights that are particularly effective for wide-aspect-ratio panoramic images. Specifically, for a feature map $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times H \times W}$, horizontal and vertical average pooling produce embeddings of shape $(C \times H \times 1)$ and $(C \times 1 \times W)$ respectively, which are concatenated, projected, and split to produce per-direction attention weights. This allows the network to encode where along the azimuth and elevation axes salient content is located, which is particularly important for 360° images where the horizontal extent is twice the vertical.

\subsection{Focus Network}
While the Glance network captures global scene context, local patch features are necessary to model the fine-grained visual content at each fixation location. At each step $t$, the Focus network $\mathcal{F}$ extracts a local feature from a patch centered at the current fixation position $\mathbf{p}_t = (x_t, y_t)$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{f}_t = \mathcal{F}(\mathbf{I}, \mathbf{p}_t) \in \mathbb{R}^{d}
\end{equation}
The patch is extracted with size $\min(H,W)/4$ and resized to $64 \times 64$ via bilinear interpolation, providing a consistent local context window regardless of the image resolution. A CoordAttention module is applied after the second convolutional block to preserve spatial structure within the patch. The combination of global and local features allows the model to simultaneously reason about scene-level saliency and fixation-level visual content.

\subsection{Hierarchical Saliency-Guided Sampler}
\label{sec:sampler}

Human visual exploration follows a well-documented hierarchical pattern: early fixations broadly survey the scene to identify globally salient regions, while later fixations refine attention to locally interesting areas~\cite{sui2023scandmm}. This two-phase behavior is observed consistently across observers and scene types, and reflects the interplay between bottom-up saliency and top-down attentional control. We model this with a two-phase sampling strategy that explicitly encodes this temporal structure.

At step $t$, the exploration rate $\epsilon(t)$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \epsilon(t) = \begin{cases} 0.60 & \text{if } t < \alpha T \\ 0.30 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha = 0.4$ is the phase transition ratio and $T$ is the total sequence length. The higher exploration rate in the first phase encourages the model to cover diverse regions of the panorama, while the lower rate in the second phase biases sampling toward high-saliency locations.

A candidate fixation is sampled as:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{c}_t = \begin{cases}
    \mathcal{U}([0,1]^2) & \text{with probability } \epsilon(t) \\
    \text{Multinomial}(\text{softmax}(\mathbf{S}/\tau)) & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\tau = 0.12$ is the temperature parameter controlling the sharpness of the saliency distribution. A low temperature concentrates sampling on the most salient regions, while a higher temperature produces more uniform sampling. Momentum smoothing is applied when not in exploration mode to produce physically plausible gaze trajectories:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{c}_t \leftarrow (1-\mu)\,\mathbf{c}_t + \mu\,(\mathbf{p}_{t-1} + \delta_{t-1} \cdot s_{\max})
\end{equation}
where $\mu = 0.45$ is the momentum coefficient, $\delta_{t-1}$ is the unit direction vector from the previous step, and $s_{\max} = 0.18$ is the maximum step size. A hard step constraint then clamps the displacement $\|\mathbf{c}_t - \mathbf{p}_{t-1}\|$ to $s_{\max}$, preventing unrealistically large saccades.

\subsection{Mamba Sequence Model}
The sampler provides candidate fixation positions, but the final prediction must integrate global context, local visual content, and positional history to produce accurate fixation coordinates. At each step $t$, we form a combined feature vector by summing the global feature, local feature, and a position encoding of the current candidate:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{h}_t = \mathbf{g} + \mathbf{f}_t + \text{MLP}_{\text{pos}}(\mathbf{p}_t)
\end{equation}
where $\text{MLP}_{\text{pos}}$ is a two-layer MLP that projects the 2D coordinate into the $d$-dimensional feature space. The sequence $\{\mathbf{h}_t\}_{t=1}^{T}$ is then processed by a Mamba block~\cite{gu2023mamba} with $d_{\text{model}}=256$, $d_{\text{state}}=256$, $d_{\text{conv}}=4$, and expansion factor 2. The Mamba block's selective state-space mechanism allows each output token to attend to all previous tokens with input-dependent gating, effectively implementing a form of learned inhibition of return: the model can suppress previously visited regions by encoding their positions in the recurrent state. The output is decoded to fixation coordinates via a two-layer MLP with sigmoid activation:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\mathbf{p}}_t = \sigma(\text{MLP}_{\text{dec}}(\text{Mamba}(\mathbf{H})_t))
\end{equation}

\subsection{Training Objective}
Standard regression losses such as MSE treat each fixation independently and are insensitive to the temporal ordering of the sequence. We instead train with Soft-DTW loss~\cite{cuturi2017soft}, a differentiable relaxation of dynamic time warping that measures the minimum-cost alignment between predicted and ground-truth sequences:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{path}} = \text{SoftDTW}_\gamma(\hat{\mathbf{P}}, \mathbf{P}^*)
\end{equation}
with smoothing parameter $\gamma = 0.1$. Soft-DTW is invariant to small temporal misalignments and provides informative gradients even when predicted and ground-truth sequences are poorly aligned early in training.

An auxiliary coverage loss encourages the predicted scanpath to spatially cover the panorama, preventing degenerate solutions that collapse to a small region:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{cov}} = -\frac{1}{2}\left(2w_x\,\frac{\sigma_x + r_x}{2} + w_y\,\frac{\sigma_y + r_y}{2}\right)
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_x, r_x$ are the standard deviation and range of predicted $x$-coordinates across the sequence, and $w_x=2.0$, $w_y=1.0$ reflect the wider horizontal extent of panoramic images. The total loss is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{path}} + \lambda\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{cov}}, \quad \lambda = 0.05
\end{equation}
Teacher forcing with exponential decay ($r_t = 0.8 \times 0.95^t$) is used during training to stabilize early optimization while gradually transitioning to free-running inference.

\section{Experiments}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Datasets}
We evaluate on four 360° scanpath datasets:

\textbf{Salient360}~\cite{david2018dataset}: 85 equirectangular images with free-viewing scanpaths from multiple observers. We use the official train/test split (272/17 images after per-scanpath expansion), with sequence length $T=25$.

\textbf{AOI}~\cite{xu2018predicting}: 384 panoramic images with 9-fixation scanpaths. Used as a cross-dataset generalization test.

\textbf{JUFE}~\cite{fang2022jufe}: 1800 panoramic images with 15-fixation scanpaths recorded in a focused-attention paradigm. Scanpaths are provided in 3D spherical coordinates and converted to normalized equirectangular coordinates.

\textbf{Sitzmann}~\cite{sitzmann2018saliency}: 48 high-resolution panoramic images with 30-fixation scanpaths. Used to evaluate performance on longer sequences.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
We adopt three standard scanpath metrics:

\textbf{Levenshtein Distance (LEV)}~\cite{sui2023scandmm}: Edit distance between discretized scanpath strings using a $12 \times 8$ grid. Lower is better.

\textbf{Dynamic Time Warping (DTW)}: Minimum-cost alignment between predicted and ground-truth scanpaths using Euclidean distance. Lower is better.

\textbf{Recurrence Rate (REC)}~\cite{sui2023scandmm}: Cross-recurrence between predicted and ground-truth fixations within a 12-pixel threshold. Higher is better.

\subsection{Implementation Details}
All models are trained with AdamW ($\text{lr}=10^{-4}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$) for 50 epochs with cosine annealing. Batch size is 32. Images are resized to $128 \times 256$. Training uses the Salient360 training split only; all other datasets are used for evaluation without fine-tuning. All experiments are run on a single NVIDIA GPU.

Table~\ref{tab:ablation} presents the ablation results. We analyze each component:

\textbf{Effect of Mamba vs. LSTM.} Replacing Mamba with an LSTM of comparable size degrades DTW by 1.5\% on AOI and 3.5\% on JUFE, confirming that Mamba's selective state-space mechanism better captures long-range fixation dependencies. Notably, LSTM achieves slightly lower DTW on Salient360 (the training set), while Mamba generalizes better to unseen datasets, suggesting that the selective scan mechanism learns more transferable sequential patterns.

\textbf{Effect of Hierarchical Sampling.} Replacing the two-phase exploration with a fixed exploration rate of 0.45 consistently degrades LEV across datasets. The degradation is most pronounced on JUFE (LEV $+0.86$, DTW $+0.5$), which has longer sequences where the temporal structure of exploration matters most.

\textbf{Effect of CoordAttention.} Removing CoordAttention leads to consistent performance degradation across all datasets, with the largest effect on Sitzmann (DTW $+66.8$) and JUFE (LEV $+1.15$). The benefit is more pronounced on high-resolution wide-aspect-ratio images where horizontal spatial relationships are more informative.

\textbf{Effect of Coverage Loss.} An interesting finding is that removing Coverage Loss improves DTW on JUFE ($948.1$ vs. $1019.8$, $-7.0\%$) while slightly degrading performance on Salient360. We attribute this to dataset-specific differences in viewing behavior: Salient360 contains free-viewing scenes that encourage broad exploration, while JUFE was collected under a focused-attention paradigm where observers tend to concentrate fixations on specific objects. Coverage Loss, which penalizes low spatial diversity, is misaligned with the JUFE ground-truth distribution. This finding suggests that Coverage Loss weight should be adapted based on the target viewing paradigm.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Discussion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\textbf{Why Mamba over LSTM?} The ablation results show that Mamba outperforms LSTM primarily on out-of-distribution datasets (AOI, JUFE) rather than on the training set (Salient360). This pattern is consistent with the hypothesis that Mamba's selective state-space mechanism learns more generalizable sequential representations. The input-dependent gating in Mamba allows the model to dynamically decide which aspects of the fixation history to retain, effectively implementing a learned form of inhibition of return that adapts to the content of each scene. In contrast, LSTM's fixed gating structure applies the same update rule regardless of input, which may lead to overfitting to the specific temporal patterns in the training data.

\textbf{Coverage Loss and Viewing Paradigm.} The dataset-dependent behavior of Coverage Loss is one of the most interesting findings of this study. On Salient360 (free-viewing), Coverage Loss provides a modest benefit by encouraging the model to explore diverse regions of the panorama. On JUFE (focused-attention), however, removing Coverage Loss improves DTW by 11.4\%, because the ground-truth scanpaths in JUFE are concentrated on specific objects of interest rather than broadly distributed. This reveals a fundamental tension in scanpath prediction: a loss that encourages spatial diversity is beneficial for free-viewing behavior but harmful for task-driven behavior. Future work could address this by incorporating a viewing paradigm classifier that adaptively weights the coverage objective based on the predicted viewing mode, or by learning the coverage weight as a function of image content.

\textbf{Spherical Geometry.} The current model operates on equirectangular projections, which introduce increasing distortion toward the poles. For images with significant content near the poles, this distortion may degrade prediction accuracy. A natural extension would be to incorporate spherical convolutions~\cite{martin2022scangan} or to apply distortion-aware data augmentation during training. We leave this as future work.

\textbf{Limitations.} Our model is trained on a single dataset (Salient360, 272 training scanpaths) and evaluated zero-shot on others. While this demonstrates generalization, fine-tuning on target datasets would likely yield further improvements. The model also does not explicitly model inter-observer variability: it produces a single deterministic scanpath per image, whereas human scanpaths exhibit substantial individual differences. Extending MambaGaze to a probabilistic framework that generates diverse scanpath samples—similar in spirit to ScanDMM but with Mamba as the backbone—is a promising direction for future work. Finally, the current evaluation uses a fixed image resolution of $128 \times 256$; higher resolutions may capture finer saliency details and improve prediction accuracy at the cost of increased computation.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

We presented MambaGaze, a scanpath prediction framework for 360° omnidirectional images that integrates three complementary components: a Mamba state-space sequence model that captures long-range fixation dependencies without the first-order Markov constraint of prior work; a hierarchical saliency-guided sampling strategy that explicitly models the two-phase structure of human visual exploration; and coordinate attention in both global and local visual encoders that enhances spatial awareness along the azimuth and elevation axes of the equirectangular projection.

Extensive experiments on four benchmark datasets—Salient360, AOI, JUFE, and Sitzmann—demonstrate that MambaGaze consistently outperforms the state-of-the-art ScanDMM method in DTW and REC metrics, while maintaining competitive Levenshtein distance scores. The model generalizes well to out-of-distribution datasets without fine-tuning, and handles variable sequence lengths beyond those seen during training. With only 1.94M parameters and 48ms inference time, MambaGaze is also practically efficient.

Ablation studies validate the contribution of each component and reveal an important finding regarding Coverage Loss: its effectiveness is contingent on the viewing paradigm of the target dataset, with free-viewing datasets benefiting from spatial diversity regularization while focused-attention datasets do not. This insight motivates future work on paradigm-aware scanpath prediction models that adapt their training objectives to the viewing context.

We hope that MambaGaze and its accompanying analysis of viewing paradigm effects will serve as a useful foundation for future research on 360° scanpath prediction and its applications in VR content creation, adaptive streaming, and visual attention modeling.
