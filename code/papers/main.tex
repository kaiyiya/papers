\begin{abstract}
Scanpath prediction for 360$^\circ$ omnidirectional images aims to generate dynamic gaze sequences that reflect human visual perception. However, many existing methods do not fully capture the temporal structure of viewing behavior and thus exhibit limited generalization across datasets. In this paper, we present a scanpath prediction framework for 360$^\circ$ images, termed \textbf{HSM} (Hierarchical Scanpath Mamba), which integrates Mamba state-space models with a hierarchical saliency-guided sampling strategy. We design a two-phase hierarchical sampler that begins with a high-exploration global phase for broad scene coverage and transitions to a low-exploration refinement phase focused on locally salient regions. Coordinate attention is incorporated into both global and local visual encoders to enhance spatial awareness along the azimuth and elevation axes of the equirectangular projection, and a Mamba-based sequence model captures long-range fixation dependencies through its selective state-space mechanism. Extensive experiments on four 360$^\circ$ scanpath benchmarks (Salient360, AOI, JUFE, and Sitzmann) show that HSM achieves state-of-the-art DTW and REC scores across all four benchmarks while remaining computationally efficient. Ablation studies further validate the contribution of each component and reveal how hierarchical exploration and coverage regularization interact with different viewing paradigms.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

360$^\circ$ images, also referred to as omnidirectional or equirectangular panoramic images, have become a prevalent form of visual content in virtual reality (VR) and augmented reality (AR) applications, providing immersive experiences in virtual environments. Understanding how people explore these panoramic scenes is crucial for optimizing user experience in VR/AR systems. Accurately predicting human eye fixation movements enables tracking and understanding of visual viewing behavior, thereby supporting more natural human-computer interaction. Estimating fixation locations also enhances foveated rendering performance and reduces computational overhead. More importantly, by analyzing human fixation data, VR/AR systems can predict users' regions of interest and intentions, which can be used to optimize interface design and provide personalized recommendations. Therefore, accurately predicting scanpaths is crucial for enhancing the immersive experience of VR/AR applications.

Scanpath prediction aims to generate a plausible sequence of fixation points that approximates the gaze trajectory of a human observer viewing a given image. Compared with fixation prediction, which only estimates where people look, scanpath prediction is more challenging because it requires predicting not only the fixation positions but also the temporal order of fixations. Unlike conventional 2D images, 360$^\circ$ panoramic images present unique perceptual challenges: the equirectangular field of view spans the full $360^\circ \times 180^\circ$ sphere, viewers actively rotate their heads to explore the scene, and the resulting gaze trajectories—known as scanpaths—exhibit complex, non-stationary spatial patterns that vary substantially across observers and scenes. Viewing conditions such as the starting point of viewing have an important impact on human scanpaths, leading to diverse trajectories among different observers. This is inherently different from 2D viewing, where observers can directly guide attention to regions of interest without head rotation. Therefore, scanpath prediction for 360$^\circ$ images is a more complex task that requires effectively modeling the time-dependency of viewing behavior.

Current 360$^\circ$ image scanpath prediction methods can be roughly divided into two categories: saliency-based methods and generative-based methods. For saliency-based methods, they usually adopt a sampling strategy to sample fixations from a predicted saliency map. However, the performance of such methods often heavily relies on the predicted saliency map and requires a satisfactory sampling strategy to interpret time-dependent visual viewing behavior. As shown in previous studies, inferior sampling strategies directly result in unstable viewing behavior, such as larger displacements and sparse fixation clusters. As for generative-based methods, they usually utilize various generative adversarial networks (GANs) to predict realistic scanpaths. Although such methods alleviate the displacement bias issue compared to saliency-based methods, they rarely focus on salient regions and face challenges in generating scanpaths of varying lengths, usually suffering from unstable training.

None of the above-mentioned methods give a complete treatment of the time-dependency of viewing behavior, which is critical for modeling dynamic gaze behaviors in 360$^\circ$ images. For time-series data, a popular approach is to leverage sequential models such as recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and long short-term memory (LSTM) networks as backbones to capture temporal relationships in scanpath prediction. However, such deterministic methods suffer from overfitting on small omnidirectional image datasets and struggle to capture long-range temporal dependencies across multiple fixations. The current state of the art, ScanDMM~\cite{sui2023scandmm}, proposes a deep Markov model that maintains a latent visual state $\mathbf{z}_t$ encoding a dynamic attention landscape, updated via a semantic-guided transition function and trained with a variational evidence lower bound (ELBO) objective. ScanDMM achieves strong performance across multiple benchmarks, but its first-order Markov structure constrains each state to depend only on the immediately preceding state, limiting the model's ability to capture long-range fixation dependencies. Furthermore, the variational training objective introduces considerable complexity, requiring careful tuning of KL annealing schedules and posterior approximations. More recently, ScanDTM~\cite{zhu2025scandtm} proposes a dual-temporal modulation framework that combines a dual graph convolutional network (Dual-GCN) with a diffusion-guided saliency module, achieving strong generalization performance. However, the diffusion-based saliency generation introduces significant computational overhead and model complexity. Therefore, there is an urgent need for a scanpath prediction model that achieves strong generalization with superior temporal modeling capabilities while remaining computationally efficient.

State space models (SSMs), and in particular the recently proposed Mamba architecture, offer a compelling alternative for sequential modeling. Mamba employs an input-dependent selective scan mechanism that dynamically gates which information to retain or discard at each step, enabling efficient modeling of long-range dependencies with linear computational complexity in sequence length. Unlike Transformers, Mamba does not require quadratic self-attention computation, making it practical for real-time inference. Unlike RNNs, its parallel training formulation avoids vanishing gradients. These properties make Mamba well-suited for scanpath prediction, where fixation sequences can span 15–30 steps and long-range context (e.g., remembering which regions have already been visited) is essential for realistic trajectory generation.

However, directly applying Mamba to scanpath prediction ignores a key characteristic of human visual exploration: its hierarchical, two-phase structure. Neuroscience and eye-tracking studies consistently show that observers first conduct a rapid global survey of the scene, directing fixations toward broadly salient regions, before transitioning to a more focused refinement phase in which attention is concentrated on locally interesting areas. This two-phase behavior is observed consistently across observers and scene types, and reflects the interplay between bottom-up saliency and top-down attentional control. A model that treats all fixation steps uniformly fails to capture this natural progression from global to local exploration, which is crucial for generating scanpaths that align with human visual behavior.

In this paper, we propose \textbf{HSM} (Hierarchical Scanpath Mamba), a scanpath prediction framework that integrates Mamba state-space models with a biologically motivated hierarchical sampling strategy for 360$^\circ$ panoramic images. Our approach makes three key contributions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item We propose a \textbf{hierarchical saliency-guided sampling strategy} that explicitly models two-phase visual exploration: a high-exploration phase (60\% random exploration rate) covering the first 40\% of fixations for broad scene coverage, followed by a low-exploration phase (30\% rate) for the remaining fixations to refine attention on salient regions. Momentum-based trajectory smoothing ensures physically plausible step sizes and smooth gaze transitions, enabling the model to generate scanpaths that closely align with human visual behavior.

  \item We incorporate \textbf{coordinate attention} into both the global Glance encoder and the local Focus encoder, decomposing spatial attention into horizontal and vertical 1D encodings. This enables the model to precisely localize salient regions along both the azimuth and elevation axes of the equirectangular projection, which carry distinct semantic meaning in 360$^\circ$ viewing.

  \item We present a \textbf{Mamba-based sequence model} that avoids the first-order Markov constraint of prior deep Markov models by adopting a selective state-space formulation, allowing each fixation prediction to be conditioned on the full history of previous fixations. The model is trained end-to-end with a differentiable Soft-DTW loss, avoiding the complexity of variational inference while achieving strong temporal modeling.
\end{itemize}

We evaluate HSM on four publicly available 360$^\circ$ scanpath benchmarks: Salient360, AOI, JUFE, and Sitzmann. The model is trained exclusively on Salient360 and evaluated zero-shot on the remaining three datasets, demonstrating strong cross-dataset generalization. Across these benchmarks, HSM attains state-of-the-art DTW and REC scores relative to representative saliency-based and generative baselines. Comprehensive ablation studies validate the contribution of each proposed component, and efficiency analysis confirms that the Mamba backbone achieves these results without increasing inference latency relative to a comparable LSTM baseline.

\section{Related Work}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Saliency-Based Methods}
Visual saliency prediction has a long history in computational vision. Early bottom-up models identified low-level features such as color, contrast, and orientation as drivers of visual attention. Deep learning approaches significantly improved saliency prediction accuracy, and several works extended these to 360$^\circ$ images by accounting for spherical distortion. However, saliency maps provide only a spatial probability distribution over fixation locations and do not model the temporal ordering of fixations. SaltiNet attempted to bridge this gap by sampling scanpaths from a time-dependent saliency volume, but the resulting paths exhibit unstable behavior with large displacements and sparse fixation clusters.

\subsection{Scanpath Prediction}
Scanpath prediction models the sequential nature of eye movements. IOR-ROI introduced inhibition of return and region-of-interest mechanisms to suppress previously visited locations. PathGAN used generative adversarial networks to produce diverse scanpaths for 2D images, later adapted to 360$^\circ$ images. ScanGAN addressed spherical geometry by incorporating spherical CNNs and CoordConv layers with a spherical distance loss.

The most closely related prior work is ScanDMM~\cite{sui2023scandmm}, which proposes a deep Markov model for 360$^\circ$ scanpath prediction. ScanDMM maintains a latent visual state $\mathbf{z}_t$ that encodes a dynamic attention landscape, updated via a semantic-guided transition function $p_{\theta_t}(\mathbf{z}_t|\mathbf{z}_{t-1})$ and trained with a variational ELBO objective. A state initialization strategy anchors the starting fixation to the most salient region. While ScanDMM achieves strong performance, its first-order Markov structure limits the model to conditioning only on the immediately preceding state, and the variational training objective introduces significant complexity.

More recently, ScanDTM~\cite{zhu2025scandtm} proposes a dual-temporal modulation framework combining a Dual-GCN encoder—comprising a semantic-level GCN for intra-image spatial relations and an image-level GCN for cross-image similarity—with a diffusion-guided saliency module that formulates saliency prediction as a conditional generative process. ScanDTM achieves strong generalization performance, but the diffusion-based saliency generation introduces substantial computational overhead. Our work replaces the Markov chain with a Mamba SSM that directly models the full fixation history through selective state updates, and replaces the ELBO with a simpler Soft-DTW objective, achieving competitive performance with significantly lower model complexity.

\subsection{State Space Models}
Structured state space models (S4) demonstrated strong performance on long-range sequence tasks by parameterizing continuous-time dynamics. Mamba extended SSMs with input-dependent selective state transitions, enabling the model to focus on relevant context while filtering out noise, achieving competitive performance with Transformers at linear computational cost. Mamba has been applied to image recognition, video understanding, and medical image segmentation, but its application to scanpath prediction remains unexplored prior to this work.

\subsection{Attention Mechanisms for Spatial Features}
Coordinate Attention decomposes channel attention into two 1D spatial encodings along height and width, enabling precise localization of spatial features. Unlike squeeze-and-excitation, which aggregates global spatial information into a single descriptor, CoordAttention preserves directional spatial context. This is particularly beneficial for 360$^\circ$ panoramic images where horizontal (azimuth) and vertical (elevation) directions carry distinct semantic meaning related to viewing behavior.

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
Given a 360$^\circ$ panoramic image $\mathbf{I} \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times H \times W}$ and its corresponding saliency map $\mathbf{S} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times H \times W}$, we aim to predict a scanpath $\hat{\mathbf{P}} = \{(\hat{x}_t, \hat{y}_t)\}_{t=1}^{T}$, where each $(\hat{x}_t, \hat{y}_t) \in [0,1]^2$ represents a normalized fixation coordinate in the equirectangular projection.

\subsection{Glance Network}
The Glance network $\mathcal{G}$ extracts a global scene representation from the full panoramic image. A global context vector is essential because human observers form an initial holistic impression of a scene before directing attention to specific regions—a phenomenon known as the ``gist'' of a scene. The Glance network consists of a convolutional encoder with two CoordAttention modules inserted after the second and third convolutional blocks:

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/coordatt.pdf}
  % TODO: 替换为实际 CoordAttention 模块图
  \fbox{\parbox{0.9\linewidth}{\centering\vspace{2.2cm}[Figure 3: CoordAttention module. Horizontal and vertical 1D pooling branches produce direction-aware attention weights $a_h$ and $a_w$, which are multiplied with the input feature map.]\vspace{2.2cm}}}
  \caption{Illustration of the CoordAttention module. The feature map is pooled along the height ($\leftrightarrow$, azimuth) and width ($\updownarrow$, elevation) axes independently, projected through a shared Conv1$\times$1, then split to produce per-direction attention weights $a_h$ and $a_w$.}
  \label{fig:coordatt}
\end{figure}
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{g} = \mathcal{G}(\mathbf{I}) \in \mathbb{R}^{d}
\end{equation}
where $d=256$ is the feature dimension. As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:coordatt}, CoordAttention decomposes the attention into horizontal and vertical pooling branches, producing spatially-aware channel weights that are particularly effective for wide-aspect-ratio panoramic images. Specifically, for a feature map $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times H \times W}$, horizontal and vertical average pooling produce embeddings of shape $(C \times H \times 1)$ and $(C \times 1 \times W)$ respectively, which are concatenated, projected, and split to produce per-direction attention weights. This allows the network to encode where along the azimuth and elevation axes salient content is located, which is particularly important for 360° images where the horizontal extent is twice the vertical.

\subsection{Focus Network}
While the Glance network captures global scene context, local patch features are necessary to model the fine-grained visual content at each fixation location. At each step $t$, the Focus network $\mathcal{F}$ extracts a local feature from a patch centered at the current fixation position $\mathbf{p}_t = (x_t, y_t)$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{f}_t = \mathcal{F}(\mathbf{I}, \mathbf{p}_t) \in \mathbb{R}^{d}
\end{equation}
The patch is extracted with size $\min(H,W)/4$ and resized to $64 \times 64$ via bilinear interpolation, providing a consistent local context window regardless of the image resolution. A CoordAttention module is applied after the second convolutional block to preserve spatial structure within the patch. The combination of global and local features allows the model to simultaneously reason about scene-level saliency and fixation-level visual content.

\subsection{Hierarchical Saliency-Guided Sampler}
\label{sec:sampler}

Human visual exploration follows a well-documented hierarchical pattern: early fixations broadly survey the scene to identify globally salient regions, while later fixations refine attention to locally interesting areas. This two-phase behavior is observed consistently across observers and scene types, and reflects the interplay between bottom-up saliency and top-down attentional control. We model this with a two-phase sampling strategy that explicitly encodes this temporal structure, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:sampler}.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/sampler.pdf}
  % TODO: 替换为实际分层采样示意图
  \fbox{\parbox{0.9\linewidth}{\centering\vspace{2.5cm}[Figure 4: Hierarchical sampling strategy. Phase 1 (left, blue): broad random exploration across the full panorama. Phase 2 (right, orange): saliency-guided refinement concentrated on salient regions. Timeline bar shows the 40/60 phase split.]\vspace{2.5cm}}}
  \caption{Illustration of the hierarchical saliency-guided sampling strategy. In Phase~1 ($t < 0.4T$, $\epsilon=0.60$), fixations spread broadly across the panorama. In Phase~2 ($t \geq 0.4T$, $\epsilon=0.30$), sampling concentrates on high-saliency regions. Momentum smoothing produces physically plausible gaze transitions between consecutive fixations.}
  \label{fig:sampler}
\end{figure}

At step $t$, the exploration rate $\epsilon(t)$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \epsilon(t) = \begin{cases} 0.60 & \text{if } t < \alpha T \\ 0.30 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha = 0.4$ is the phase transition ratio and $T$ is the total sequence length. The higher exploration rate in the first phase encourages the model to cover diverse regions of the panorama, while the lower rate in the second phase biases sampling toward high-saliency locations.

A candidate fixation is sampled as:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{c}_t = \begin{cases}
    \mathcal{U}([0,1]^2) & \text{with probability } \epsilon(t) \\
    \text{Multinomial}(\text{softmax}(\mathbf{S}/\tau)) & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\tau = 0.12$ is the temperature parameter controlling the sharpness of the saliency distribution. A low temperature concentrates sampling on the most salient regions, while a higher temperature produces more uniform sampling. Momentum smoothing is applied when not in exploration mode to produce physically plausible gaze trajectories:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{c}_t \leftarrow (1-\mu)\,\mathbf{c}_t + \mu\,(\mathbf{p}_{t-1} + \delta_{t-1} \cdot s_{\max})
\end{equation}
where $\mu = 0.45$ is the momentum coefficient, $\delta_{t-1}$ is the unit direction vector from the previous step, and $s_{\max} = 0.18$ is the maximum step size. A hard step constraint then clamps the displacement $\|\mathbf{c}_t - \mathbf{p}_{t-1}\|$ to $s_{\max}$, preventing unrealistically large saccades.

\subsection{Mamba Sequence Model}
The sampler provides candidate fixation positions, but the final prediction must integrate global context, local visual content, and positional history to produce accurate fixation coordinates. At each step $t$, we form a combined feature vector by summing the global feature, local feature, and a position encoding of the current candidate:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{h}_t = \mathbf{g} + \mathbf{f}_t + \text{MLP}_{\text{pos}}(\mathbf{p}_t)
\end{equation}
where $\text{MLP}_{\text{pos}}$ is a two-layer MLP that projects the 2D coordinate into the $d$-dimensional feature space. The sequence $\{\mathbf{h}_t\}_{t=1}^{T}$ is then processed by a Mamba block with $d_{\text{model}}=256$, $d_{\text{state}}=256$, $d_{\text{conv}}=4$, and expansion factor 2. The Mamba block's selective state-space mechanism allows each output token to attend to all previous tokens with input-dependent gating, effectively implementing a form of learned inhibition of return: the model can suppress previously visited regions by encoding their positions in the recurrent state. The output is decoded to fixation coordinates via a two-layer MLP with sigmoid activation:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\mathbf{p}}_t = \sigma(\text{MLP}_{\text{dec}}(\text{Mamba}(\mathbf{H})_t))
\end{equation}

\subsection{Training Objective}
Standard regression losses such as MSE treat each fixation independently and are insensitive to the temporal ordering of the sequence. We instead train with Soft-DTW loss, a differentiable relaxation of dynamic time warping that measures the minimum-cost alignment between predicted and ground-truth sequences:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{path}} = \text{SoftDTW}_\gamma(\hat{\mathbf{P}}, \mathbf{P}^*)
\end{equation}
with smoothing parameter $\gamma = 0.1$. Soft-DTW is invariant to small temporal misalignments and provides informative gradients even when predicted and ground-truth sequences are poorly aligned early in training.

An auxiliary coverage loss encourages the predicted scanpath to spatially cover the panorama, preventing degenerate solutions that collapse to a small region:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{cov}} = -\frac{1}{2}\left(2w_x\,\frac{\sigma_x + r_x}{2} + w_y\,\frac{\sigma_y + r_y}{2}\right)
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_x, r_x$ are the standard deviation and range of predicted $x$-coordinates across the sequence, and $w_x=2.0$, $w_y=1.0$ reflect the wider horizontal extent of panoramic images. The total loss is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{path}} + \lambda\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{cov}}, \quad \lambda = 0.05
\end{equation}
Teacher forcing with exponential decay ($r_t = 0.8 \times 0.95^t$) is used during training to stabilize early optimization while gradually transitioning to free-running inference.

\section{Experiments}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Datasets}
We evaluate on four 360$^\circ$ scanpath datasets:

\textbf{Salient360}: 85 equirectangular images with free-viewing scanpaths from multiple observers. We use the official train/test split (272/17 images after per-scanpath expansion), with sequence length $T=25$.

\textbf{AOI}: 384 panoramic images with 9-fixation scanpaths. Used as a cross-dataset generalization test.

\textbf{JUFE}: 1800 panoramic images with 15-fixation scanpaths recorded in a focused-attention paradigm. Scanpaths are provided in 3D spherical coordinates and converted to normalized equirectangular coordinates.

\textbf{Sitzmann}: 48 high-resolution panoramic images with 30-fixation scanpaths. Used to evaluate performance on longer sequences.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
We adopt three standard scanpath metrics:

\textbf{Levenshtein Distance (LEV)}: Edit distance between discretized scanpath strings using a $12 \times 8$ grid. Lower is better.

\textbf{Dynamic Time Warping (DTW)}: Minimum-cost alignment between predicted and ground-truth scanpaths using Euclidean distance. Lower is better.

\textbf{Recurrence Rate (REC)}: Cross-recurrence between predicted and ground-truth fixations within a 12-pixel threshold. Higher is better.

\subsection{Implementation Details}
All models are trained with AdamW ($\text{lr}=10^{-4}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$) for 50 epochs with cosine annealing. Batch size is 128. Images are resized to $128 \times 256$. Training uses the Salient360 training split only; all other datasets are used for evaluation without fine-tuning. All experiments are run on a single NVIDIA GPU.

This suggests that the hierarchical sampling strategy and Mamba sequence model learn viewing patterns that transfer well across different panoramic image content and observer populations.

This demonstrates that the model can generalize to sequence lengths beyond those seen during training, a property that is important for practical deployment where the desired scanpath length may vary.

The qualitative comparison in Figure~\ref{fig:qualitative} illustrates that HSM produces scanpaths that cover salient regions more systematically, with smoother transitions between fixations and less tendency to cluster in the image center compared to baseline methods.

\section{Discussion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\textbf{Why Mamba over LSTM?} The ablation results show that Mamba outperforms LSTM primarily on out-of-distribution datasets (AOI, JUFE) rather than on the training set (Salient360). This pattern is consistent with the hypothesis that Mamba's selective state-space mechanism learns more generalizable sequential representations. The input-dependent gating in Mamba allows the model to dynamically decide which aspects of the fixation history to retain, effectively implementing a learned form of inhibition of return that adapts to the content of each scene. In contrast, LSTM's fixed gating structure applies the same update rule regardless of input, which may lead to overfitting to the specific temporal patterns in the training data.

\textbf{Coverage Loss and Viewing Paradigm.} The dataset-dependent behavior of Coverage Loss is one of the most interesting findings of this study. On Salient360 (free-viewing), Coverage Loss provides a modest benefit by encouraging the model to explore diverse regions of the panorama. On JUFE (focused-attention), however, removing Coverage Loss improves DTW by about 7\% (from 1019.8 to 948.1), because the ground-truth scanpaths in JUFE are concentrated on specific objects of interest rather than broadly distributed. This reveals a fundamental tension in scanpath prediction: a loss that encourages spatial diversity is beneficial for free-viewing behavior but harmful for task-driven behavior. Future work could address this by incorporating a viewing paradigm classifier that adaptively weights the coverage objective based on the predicted viewing mode, or by learning the coverage weight as a function of image content.

\textbf{Spherical Geometry.} The current model operates on equirectangular projections, which introduce increasing distortion toward the poles. For images with significant content near the poles, this distortion may degrade prediction accuracy. A natural extension would be to incorporate spherical convolutions or to apply distortion-aware data augmentation during training. We leave this as future work.

\textbf{Limitations.} Our model is trained on a single dataset (Salient360, 272 training scanpaths) and evaluated zero-shot on others. While this demonstrates generalization, fine-tuning on target datasets would likely yield further improvements. The model also does not explicitly model inter-observer variability: it produces a single deterministic scanpath per image, whereas human scanpaths exhibit substantial individual differences. Extending HSM to a probabilistic framework that generates diverse scanpath samples—similar in spirit to recent probabilistic models but with Mamba as the backbone—is a promising direction for future work. Finally, the current evaluation uses a fixed image resolution of $128 \times 256$; higher resolutions may capture finer saliency details and improve prediction accuracy at the cost of increased computation.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

We presented HSM, a scanpath prediction framework for 360$^\circ$ omnidirectional images that integrates three complementary components: a Mamba state-space sequence model that captures long-range fixation dependencies without the first-order Markov constraint of prior work such as ScanDMM; a hierarchical saliency-guided sampling strategy that explicitly models the two-phase structure of human visual exploration; and coordinate attention in both global and local visual encoders that enhances spatial awareness along the azimuth and elevation axes of the equirectangular projection.

Extensive experiments on four benchmark datasets—Salient360, AOI, JUFE, and Sitzmann—demonstrate that HSM achieves state-of-the-art DTW and REC scores compared with representative saliency-based and generative methods including ScanDMM~\cite{sui2023scandmm} and ScanDTM~\cite{zhu2025scandtm}. The model generalizes well to out-of-distribution datasets without fine-tuning and handles variable sequence lengths beyond those seen during training. With only 1.94M parameters and 48ms inference time, HSM is also practically efficient.

Ablation studies validate the contribution of each component and reveal an important finding regarding Coverage Loss: its effectiveness is contingent on the viewing paradigm of the target dataset, with free-viewing datasets benefiting from spatial diversity regularization while focused-attention datasets do not. This insight motivates future work on paradigm-aware scanpath prediction models that adapt their training objectives to the viewing context.

We hope that HSM and its accompanying analysis of viewing paradigm effects will serve as a useful foundation for future research on 360$^\circ$ scanpath prediction and its applications in VR content creation, adaptive streaming, and visual attention modeling.
