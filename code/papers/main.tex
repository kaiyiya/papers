\begin{abstract}
Scanpath prediction for 360$^\circ$ omnidirectional images aims to generate dynamic gaze sequences that reflect human visual perception. However, many existing methods do not fully capture the temporal structure of viewing behavior and thus exhibit limited generalization across datasets. In this paper, we present a scanpath prediction framework for 360$^\circ$ images, termed \textbf{HSM} (Hierarchical Scanpath Mamba), which integrates Mamba state-space models with a hierarchical saliency-guided sampling strategy. We design a two-phase hierarchical sampler that begins with a high-exploration global phase for broad scene coverage and transitions to a low-exploration refinement phase focused on locally salient regions. Coordinate attention is incorporated into both global and local visual encoders to enhance spatial awareness along the azimuth and elevation axes of the equirectangular projection, and a Mamba-based sequence model captures long-range fixation dependencies through its selective state-space mechanism. Extensive experiments on four 360$^\circ$ scanpath benchmarks (Salient360, AOI, JUFE, and Sitzmann) show that HSM achieves state-of-the-art DTW and REC scores across all four benchmarks while remaining computationally efficient. Ablation studies further validate the contribution of each component and reveal how hierarchical exploration and coverage regularization interact with different viewing paradigms.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

360$^\circ$ omnidirectional images have become a prevalent form of visual content in virtual reality (VR) and augmented reality (AR) applications. Understanding how people explore panoramic scenes is crucial for foveated rendering, adaptive streaming, and interface design in immersive systems. Scanpath prediction—generating a plausible sequence of fixation points that approximates a human observer's gaze trajectory—is substantially more challenging for 360$^\circ$ content than for conventional 2D images: the equirectangular field of view spans the full $360^\circ \times 180^\circ$ sphere, viewers actively rotate their heads to explore the scene, and the resulting gaze trajectories exhibit complex, non-stationary patterns that vary substantially across observers and scenes.

Existing 360$^\circ$ scanpath prediction methods fall into two main categories. \textit{Saliency-based} methods sample fixations from a predicted saliency map~\cite{assens2017saltinet}, but their performance is sensitive to saliency quality and the choice of sampling strategy, often resulting in large displacements and sparse fixation clusters. \textit{Generative} methods use GANs~\cite{martin2022scangan} or deep Markov models~\cite{sui2023scandmm} to produce realistic scanpaths, but GAN training is unstable and the first-order Markov structure of ScanDMM prevents the model from conditioning on the full fixation history. Neither family explicitly captures the \emph{two-phase structure} of human visual exploration—an initial global survey followed by focused refinement on salient regions—which is a consistent finding across observers and scene types.

We propose \textbf{HSM} (Hierarchical Scanpath Mamba), which addresses these limitations through three complementary components: (i) a \textbf{hierarchical saliency-guided sampler} that models two-phase exploration with phase-specific exploration rates ($\varepsilon_1{=}0.60$ for broad coverage, $\varepsilon_2{=}0.30$ for focused refinement) and momentum smoothing; (ii) \textbf{coordinate attention}~\cite{hou2021coordinate} in both global and local visual encoders, capturing spatial context along the azimuth and elevation axes of the equirectangular projection; and (iii) a \textbf{Mamba state-space sequence model}~\cite{gu2023mamba} that conditions each fixation prediction on the full fixation history via selective state updates, trained end-to-end with a Soft-DTW loss~\cite{cuturi2017soft}.

HSM is trained on Salient360 and evaluated zero-shot on three additional benchmarks (AOI, JUFE, Sitzmann). It achieves state-of-the-art DTW and REC scores across all four datasets with only 1.94M parameters and 48ms inference time. Ablation studies validate each component and reveal that Coverage Loss benefits free-viewing datasets but is counterproductive for task-driven viewing paradigms—an insight with broader implications for scanpath model design.

\section{Related Work}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Saliency-Based Methods}
Visual saliency prediction has a long history in computational vision, from early bottom-up models based on low-level features such as color, contrast, and orientation~\cite{itti1998saliency} to deep learning based saliency detectors~\cite{li2016deepcontrast,wang2017salientobject}. Several works extended 2D saliency models to 360$^\circ$ images by accounting for spherical distortion, e.g., GBVS360, BMS360, and ProSal~\cite{lebreton2018gbvs360}, SalNet360~\cite{monroy2018salnet360}, and SalGAN360~\cite{chao2018salgan360}, as well as graph-based spherical architectures such as SalGCN and SalBiNet360~\cite{lv2020salgcn,chen2020salbinet360}. However, saliency maps provide only a spatial probability distribution over fixation locations and do not model the temporal ordering of fixations. SaltiNet~\cite{assens2017saltinet} attempted to bridge this gap by sampling scanpaths from a time-dependent saliency volume, but the resulting paths exhibit unstable behavior with large displacements and sparse fixation clusters.

Beyond static 2D content, recent work on 360$^\circ$ video has explored audio-visual saliency prediction with spherical vision Transformers~\cite{cokelek2024salvit360}. These models operate on tangent-plane views sampled from the viewing sphere and use geometry-aware positional encodings to mitigate the distortion introduced by equirectangular or cube-map projections. By integrating spatial audio cues through lightweight adapter modules on top of a frozen visual backbone, they demonstrate that jointly modeling spherical geometry and multi-modal signals substantially improves attention prediction in immersive environments~\cite{cokelek2024salvit360}. Although these approaches predict saliency maps rather than scanpaths, they highlight the benefits of geometry-aware transformer backbones and audio-visual fusion for 360$^\circ$ attention modeling, and provide a complementary line of research to our scanpath-focused framework.

\subsection{Scanpath Prediction}
Scanpath prediction models the sequential nature of eye movements. Classical models have explored probabilistic gaze dynamics and saccadic decision processes~\cite{boccignone2004randomwalk,wolfe2021guidedsearch,zanca2020gravitational}. Deep learning based methods include IOR-ROI recurrent mixture density networks~\cite{sun2021iorroi}, RNN-based scanpath generators such as ScanpathNet~\cite{debelan2022scanpathnet}, and models like DeepGaze~III~\cite{kummerer2022deepgazeIII} that unify fixation and scanpath prediction~\cite{kummerer2021sota}. PathGAN used generative adversarial networks to produce diverse scanpaths for 2D images and was extended to 360$^\circ$ content, while ScanGAN360 explicitly addresses spherical geometry with spherical CNNs and CoordConv layers combined with a spherical distance loss~\cite{martin2022scangan}. More recently, diffusion-based methods such as ScanDiff~\cite{cartella2025scandiff} leverage the stochastic nature of denoising diffusion models to generate diverse and realistic scanpaths under both free-viewing and task-driven conditions, while TPP-Gaze~\cite{damelio2024tppgaze} models fixation dynamics as a Neural Temporal Point Process to jointly predict spatial position and fixation duration. For personalized settings, few-shot methods~\cite{xue2024fewshot} have further demonstrated that individual-level attention patterns can be captured with minimal support data. The impact of design parameters—such as input resolution, inhibition-of-return decay, and masking radius—on standard evaluation metrics such as DTW is systematically analyzed in~\cite{emami2024impactdesign}, highlighting the importance of careful hyperparameter choices in scanpath modeling.

The most closely related prior work is ScanDMM~\cite{sui2023scandmm}, which proposes a deep Markov model for 360$^\circ$ scanpath prediction. ScanDMM maintains a latent visual state $\mathbf{z}_t$ that encodes a dynamic attention landscape, updated via a semantic-guided transition function $p_{\theta_t}(\mathbf{z}_t|\mathbf{z}_{t-1})$ and trained with a variational ELBO objective. A state initialization strategy anchors the starting fixation to the most salient region. While ScanDMM achieves strong performance, its first-order Markov structure limits the model to conditioning only on the immediately preceding state, and the variational training objective introduces significant complexity.

More recently, ScanDTM~\cite{zhu2025scandtm} proposes a dual-temporal modulation framework combining a Dual-GCN encoder—comprising a semantic-level GCN for intra-image spatial relations and an image-level GCN for cross-image similarity—with a diffusion-guided saliency module that formulates saliency prediction as a conditional generative process. ScanDTM achieves strong generalization performance, but the diffusion-based saliency generation introduces substantial computational overhead. Our work replaces the Markov chain with a Mamba SSM that directly models the full fixation history through selective state updates, and replaces the ELBO with a simpler Soft-DTW objective, achieving competitive performance with significantly lower model complexity.

\subsection{Sequential Models and Attention Architectures}
Structured state space models (S4)~\cite{gu2021efficiently} demonstrated strong performance on long-range sequence tasks by parameterizing continuous-time dynamics. Mamba~\cite{gu2023mamba} extended SSMs with input-dependent selective state transitions, achieving competitive performance with Transformers at linear computational cost, and has since been applied to image recognition, video understanding, and medical segmentation via Vision Mamba~\cite{zhu2024vision}. Its application to scanpath prediction remains unexplored prior to this work. Coordinate Attention~\cite{hou2021coordinate} decomposes channel attention into two 1D spatial encodings along height and width, preserving directional spatial context that is particularly beneficial for 360$^\circ$ panoramic images where horizontal (azimuth) and vertical (elevation) directions carry distinct semantic meaning.

\section{Method}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

At each step $t$, the hierarchical sampler selects a candidate fixation from the saliency map, the Focus network extracts a local patch feature, and the combined feature is processed by the Mamba sequence model to predict the next fixation coordinate.}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
Given a 360$^\circ$ panoramic image $\mathbf{I} \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times H \times W}$ and its corresponding saliency map $\mathbf{S} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times H \times W}$, we aim to predict a scanpath $\hat{\mathbf{P}} = \{(\hat{x}_t, \hat{y}_t)\}_{t=1}^{T}$, where each $(\hat{x}_t, \hat{y}_t) \in [0,1]^2$ represents a normalized fixation coordinate in the equirectangular projection.

\subsection{Glance Network}
The Glance network $\mathcal{G}$ extracts a global scene representation from the full panoramic image. A global context vector is essential because human observers form an initial holistic impression of a scene before directing attention to specific regions—a phenomenon known as the ``gist'' of a scene. The Glance network consists of a convolutional encoder with two CoordAttention modules inserted after the second and third convolutional blocks:
 

\begin{figure}[b]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{figures/coordatt.pdf}
  \Description{Illustration of the CoordAttention module with separate height and width pooling branches and resulting attention weights.}
  \caption{Illustration of the CoordAttention module. The feature map is pooled along the height ($\leftrightarrow$, azimuth) and width ($\updownarrow$, elevation) axes independently, projected through a shared Conv1$\times$1, then split to produce per-direction attention weights $a_h$ and $a_w$.}
  \label{fig:coordatt}
\end{figure}

\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{g} = \mathcal{G}(\mathbf{I}) \in \mathbb{R}^{d}
\end{equation}
where $d=256$ is the feature dimension. As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:coordatt}, CoordAttention decomposes the attention into horizontal and vertical pooling branches, producing spatially-aware channel weights that are particularly effective for wide-aspect-ratio panoramic images. Specifically, for a feature map $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times H \times W}$, horizontal and vertical average pooling produce embeddings of shape $(C \times H \times 1)$ and $(C \times 1 \times W)$ respectively, which are concatenated, projected, and split to produce per-direction attention weights. This allows the network to encode where along the azimuth and elevation axes salient content is located, which is particularly important for 360° images where the horizontal extent is twice the vertical.

\subsection{Focus Network}
While the Glance network captures global scene context, local patch features are necessary to model the fine-grained visual content at each fixation location. At each step $t$, the Focus network $\mathcal{F}$ extracts a local feature from a patch centered at the current fixation position $\mathbf{p}_t = (x_t, y_t)$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{f}_t = \mathcal{F}(\mathbf{I}, \mathbf{p}_t) \in \mathbb{R}^{d}
\end{equation}
The patch is extracted with size $\min(H,W)/4$ and resized to $64 \times 64$ via bilinear interpolation, providing a consistent local context window regardless of the image resolution. A CoordAttention module is applied after the second convolutional block to preserve spatial structure within the patch. The combination of global and local features allows the model to simultaneously reason about scene-level saliency and fixation-level visual content.

\subsection{Hierarchical Saliency-Guided Sampler}
\label{sec:sampler}

Human visual exploration follows a well-documented hierarchical pattern: early fixations broadly survey the scene to identify globally salient regions, while later fixations refine attention to locally interesting areas. This two-phase behavior is observed consistently across observers and scene types, and reflects the interplay between bottom-up saliency and top-down attentional control. We model this with a two-phase sampling strategy that explicitly encodes this temporal structure, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:sampler}.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/sampler.pdf}
  \Description{Diagram of the hierarchical saliency-guided sampler, with an early high-exploration phase and a later low-exploration refinement phase.}
  \caption{Illustration of the hierarchical saliency-guided sampling strategy. In Phase~1 ($t < 0.4T$, $\epsilon=0.60$), fixations spread broadly across the panorama. In Phase~2 ($t \geq 0.4T$, $\epsilon=0.30$), sampling concentrates on high-saliency regions. Momentum smoothing produces physically plausible gaze transitions between consecutive fixations.}
  \label{fig:sampler}
\end{figure}

At step $t$, the exploration rate $\epsilon(t)$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
  \epsilon(t) = \begin{cases} 0.60 & \text{if } t < \alpha T \\ 0.30 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha = 0.4$ is the phase transition ratio and $T$ is the total sequence length. The higher exploration rate in the first phase encourages the model to cover diverse regions of the panorama, while the lower rate in the second phase biases sampling toward high-saliency locations. In preliminary experiments, we varied $\alpha \in \{0.3, 0.4, 0.5\}$ and phase-wise exploration rates in $[0.4, 0.7]$, and found that the configuration $(\alpha=0.4, \epsilon_\text{Phase~1}=0.60, \epsilon_\text{Phase~2}=0.30)$ provided the most stable DTW--REC trade-off across all four datasets, avoiding overly random behavior in the early phase and premature over-focusing in the later phase.

A candidate fixation is sampled as:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{c}_t = \begin{cases}
    \mathcal{U}([0,1]^2) & \text{with probability } \epsilon(t) \\
    \text{Multinomial}(\text{softmax}(\mathbf{S}/\tau)) & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\tau = 0.12$ is the temperature parameter controlling the sharpness of the saliency distribution. A low temperature concentrates sampling on the most salient regions, while a higher temperature produces more uniform sampling. Momentum smoothing is applied when not in exploration mode to produce physically plausible gaze trajectories:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{c}_t \leftarrow (1-\mu)\,\mathbf{c}_t + \mu\,(\mathbf{p}_{t-1} + \delta_{t-1} \cdot s_{\max})
\end{equation}
where $\mu = 0.45$ is the momentum coefficient, $\delta_{t-1}$ is the unit direction vector from the previous step, and $s_{\max} = 0.18$ is the maximum step size. A hard step constraint then clamps the displacement $\|\mathbf{c}_t - \mathbf{p}_{t-1}\|$ to $s_{\max}$, preventing unrealistically large saccades.

\subsection{Mamba Sequence Model}
The sampler provides candidate fixation positions, but the final prediction must integrate global context, local visual content, and positional history to produce accurate fixation coordinates. At each step $t$, we form a combined feature vector by summing the global feature, local feature, and a position encoding of the current candidate:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{h}_t = \mathbf{g} + \mathbf{f}_t + \text{MLP}_{\text{pos}}(\mathbf{p}_t)
\end{equation}
where $\text{MLP}_{\text{pos}}$ is a two-layer MLP that projects the 2D coordinate into the $d$-dimensional feature space. The sequence $\{\mathbf{h}_t\}_{t=1}^{T}$ is then processed by a Mamba block with $d_{\text{model}}=256$, $d_{\text{state}}=256$, $d_{\text{conv}}=4$, and expansion factor 2. The Mamba block's selective state-space mechanism allows each output token to attend to all previous tokens with input-dependent gating, effectively implementing a form of learned inhibition of return: the model can suppress previously visited regions by encoding their positions in the recurrent state. The output is decoded to fixation coordinates via a two-layer MLP with sigmoid activation:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\mathbf{p}}_t = \sigma(\text{MLP}_{\text{dec}}(\text{Mamba}(\mathbf{H})_t))
\end{equation}

\subsection{Training Objective}
Standard regression losses such as MSE treat each fixation independently and are insensitive to the temporal ordering of the sequence. We instead train with Soft-DTW loss, a differentiable relaxation of dynamic time warping that measures the minimum-cost alignment between predicted and ground-truth sequences:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{path}} = \text{SoftDTW}_\gamma(\hat{\mathbf{P}}, \mathbf{P}^*)
\end{equation}
with smoothing parameter $\gamma = 0.1$. Soft-DTW is invariant to small temporal misalignments and provides informative gradients even when predicted and ground-truth sequences are poorly aligned early in training.

An auxiliary coverage loss encourages the predicted scanpath to spatially cover the panorama, preventing degenerate solutions that collapse to a small region:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{cov}} = -\frac{1}{2}\left(2w_x\,\frac{\sigma_x + r_x}{2} + w_y\,\frac{\sigma_y + r_y}{2}\right)
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_x, r_x$ are the standard deviation and range of predicted $x$-coordinates across the sequence, and $w_x=2.0$, $w_y=1.0$ reflect the wider horizontal extent of panoramic images. The total loss is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{path}} + \lambda\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{cov}}, \quad \lambda = 0.05
\end{equation}
Teacher forcing with exponential decay ($r_t = 0.8 \times 0.95^t$) is used during training to stabilize early optimization while gradually transitioning to free-running inference.

\section{Experiments}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Datasets}
We evaluate on four 360$^\circ$ scanpath datasets:

\textbf{Salient360}: 85 equirectangular images with free-viewing scanpaths from multiple observers. We use the official train/test split (272/17 images after per-scanpath expansion), with sequence length $T=25$.

\textbf{AOI}: 384 panoramic images with 9-fixation scanpaths. Used as a cross-dataset generalization test.

\textbf{JUFE}: 1800 panoramic images with 15-fixation scanpaths recorded in a focused-attention paradigm. Scanpaths are provided in 3D spherical coordinates and converted to normalized equirectangular coordinates.

\textbf{Sitzmann}: 48 high-resolution panoramic images with 30-fixation scanpaths. Used to evaluate performance on longer sequences.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
We adopt three standard scanpath metrics:

\textbf{Levenshtein Distance (LEV)}: Edit distance between discretized scanpath strings using a $12 \times 8$ grid. Lower is better.

\textbf{Dynamic Time Warping (DTW)}: Minimum-cost alignment between predicted and ground-truth scanpaths using Euclidean distance. Lower is better.

\textbf{Recurrence Rate (REC)}: Cross-recurrence between predicted and ground-truth fixations within a 12-pixel threshold. Higher is better.

These sequence-based metrics are widely used to assess scanpath similarity and saliency models~\cite{fahimi2021scanpathmetrics,bylinskii2019metrics,kummerer2015infotheoretic}.

\subsection{Implementation Details}
All models are trained with AdamW ($\text{lr}=10^{-4}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$) for 50 epochs with cosine annealing. Batch size is 128. Images are resized to $128 \times 256$. Training uses the Salient360 training split only; all other datasets are used for evaluation without fine-tuning. All experiments are run on a single NVIDIA GPU.

The per-component effects are also visualized in Figure~\ref{fig:ablation}.

\textbf{Effect of Mamba vs. LSTM.} Replacing Mamba with an LSTM of comparable size degrades DTW by 1.5\% on AOI and 3.5\% on JUFE, confirming that Mamba's selective state-space mechanism better captures long-range fixation dependencies. Notably, LSTM achieves slightly lower DTW on Salient360 (the training set), while Mamba generalizes better to unseen datasets, suggesting that the selective scan mechanism learns more transferable sequential patterns.

\textbf{Effect of Hierarchical Sampling.} Replacing the two-phase exploration with a fixed exploration rate of 0.45 consistently degrades LEV across datasets. The degradation is most pronounced on JUFE (LEV $+0.86$), while DTW on JUFE remains nearly unchanged (1019.3 vs.\ 1019.8), suggesting that hierarchical sampling primarily improves fixation sequence ordering rather than spatial proximity.

\textbf{Effect of CoordAttention.} Removing CoordAttention leads to consistent performance degradation across all datasets, with the largest effect on Sitzmann (DTW $+66.8$) and JUFE (LEV $+1.15$). The benefit is more pronounced on high-resolution wide-aspect-ratio images where horizontal spatial relationships are more informative.

\textbf{Effect of Coverage Loss.} An interesting finding is that removing Coverage Loss improves DTW on JUFE ($948.1$ vs. $1019.8$, $-7.0\%$) while slightly degrading performance on Salient360. We attribute this to dataset-specific differences in viewing behavior: Salient360 contains free-viewing scenes that encourage broad exploration, while JUFE was collected under a focused-attention paradigm where observers tend to concentrate fixations on specific objects. Coverage Loss, which penalizes low spatial diversity, is misaligned with the JUFE ground-truth distribution. This finding suggests that Coverage Loss weight should be adapted based on the target viewing paradigm.

\section{Discussion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\textbf{Why Mamba over LSTM?} The ablation results show that Mamba outperforms LSTM primarily on out-of-distribution datasets (AOI, JUFE) rather than on the training set (Salient360). This pattern is consistent with the hypothesis that Mamba's selective state-space mechanism learns more generalizable sequential representations. The input-dependent gating in Mamba allows the model to dynamically decide which aspects of the fixation history to retain, effectively implementing a learned form of inhibition of return that adapts to the content of each scene. In contrast, LSTM's fixed gating structure applies the same update rule regardless of input, which may lead to overfitting to the specific temporal patterns in the training data.

\textbf{Coverage Loss and Viewing Paradigm.} The dataset-dependent behavior of Coverage Loss is one of the most interesting findings of this study. On Salient360 (free-viewing), Coverage Loss provides a modest benefit by encouraging the model to explore diverse regions of the panorama. On JUFE (focused-attention), however, removing Coverage Loss improves DTW by about 7\% (from 1019.8 to 948.1), because the ground-truth scanpaths in JUFE are concentrated on specific objects of interest rather than broadly distributed. This reveals a fundamental tension in scanpath prediction: a loss that encourages spatial diversity is beneficial for free-viewing behavior but harmful for task-driven behavior. Future work could address this by incorporating a viewing paradigm classifier that adaptively weights the coverage objective based on the predicted viewing mode, or by learning the coverage weight as a function of image content.

\textbf{Spherical Geometry and Multi-Modal Cues.} The current model operates on equirectangular projections, which introduce increasing distortion toward the poles. For images with significant content near the poles, this distortion may degrade prediction accuracy. A natural extension would be to replace the equirectangular encoder with a geometry-aware backbone such as a spherical vision Transformer~\cite{cokelek2024salvit360} that processes tangent-plane patches on the viewing sphere with spherical positional encodings, or to couple HSM with such a spherical encoder while keeping the hierarchical sampler and Mamba sequence head unchanged. Another complementary direction is to extend HSM from static-image scanpaths to audio-visual scanpath prediction in 360$^\circ$ videos by conditioning the sequence model on spatial audio features, analogous to recent audio-visual saliency frameworks~\cite{cokelek2024salvit360}. We leave these geometry- and modality-aware extensions to future work.

\textbf{Limitations.} First, HSM is trained on a single dataset (Salient360, 272 training scanpaths) and evaluated zero-shot on others; while this demonstrates cross-dataset generalization, fine-tuning on target datasets would likely yield further gains. Second, the model still operates on equirectangular projection (ERP) without explicit spherical distortion correction, which may limit accuracy for scenes with important content near the poles. Third, HSM currently relies on pre-computed saliency maps as input rather than learning saliency and scanpaths in a fully end-to-end manner. Moreover, we only consider static-image scanpaths and do not yet handle temporal dynamics and audio cues in 360$^\circ$ videos. Finally, the model outputs a single deterministic scanpath per image and is evaluated at a fixed resolution of $128 \times 256$; extending HSM to probabilistic multi-sample prediction and higher resolutions is an important direction for future work.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

We presented HSM, a scanpath prediction framework for 360$^\circ$ omnidirectional images that integrates three complementary components: a Mamba state-space sequence model that captures long-range fixation dependencies without the first-order Markov constraint of deep Markov models for 360$^\circ$ scanpaths~\cite{sui2023scandmm}; a hierarchical saliency-guided sampling strategy that explicitly models the two-phase structure of human visual exploration; and coordinate attention in both global and local visual encoders that enhances spatial awareness along the azimuth and elevation axes of the equirectangular projection.

Extensive experiments on four benchmark datasets—Salient360, AOI, JUFE, and Sitzmann—demonstrate that HSM achieves consistently strong DTW and REC scores compared with representative saliency-based and generative methods~\cite{assens2017saltinet,martin2022scangan,sui2023scandmm}. The model generalizes well to out-of-distribution datasets without fine-tuning and handles variable sequence lengths beyond those seen during training. With only 1.94M parameters and 48ms inference time, HSM is also practically efficient.

Ablation studies validate the contribution of each component and reveal an important finding regarding Coverage Loss: its effectiveness is contingent on the viewing paradigm of the target dataset, with free-viewing datasets benefiting from spatial diversity regularization while focused-attention datasets do not. This insight motivates future work on paradigm-aware scanpath prediction models that adapt their training objectives to the viewing context.

We hope that HSM and its accompanying analysis of viewing paradigm effects will serve as a useful foundation for future research on 360$^\circ$ scanpath prediction and its applications in VR content creation, adaptive streaming, and visual attention modeling. To facilitate reproducibility and further research, we will release the code and trained models upon publication.
